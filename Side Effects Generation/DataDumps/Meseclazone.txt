'''Meseclazone''' ('''W-2395'''), also known as '''2-methylseclazone''', is a [[nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drug]] (NSAID) developed in the 1970s.<ref name="isbn0-412-54090-8">{{cite book | author = | title = Dictionary of organic compounds | publisher = Chapman & Hall | location = London | year = 1996 | pages = | isbn = 0-412-54090-8 | oclc = | doi = | url = https://books.google.com/books?id=r7z8W69GcoMC&lpg=PA4167&dq=Meseclazone&pg=PA4167#v=onepage&q&f=false}}</ref><ref name="isbn0-7484-0885-1">{{cite book | author = Rainsford, K. D. | title = Aspirin and related drugs | publisher = Taylor & Francis | location = Washington, DC | year = 2004 | pages = | isbn = 0-7484-0885-1 | oclc = | doi = | url = https://books.google.com/books?id=9xYuQKZSDkIC&lpg=PA67&dq=Meseclazone&pg=PA67#v=onepage&q&f=false}}</ref> It functions as a [[prodrug]] to the 5-[[chloro]] [[chemical derivative|derivative]] of [[salicyclic acid]].<ref name="isbn0-7484-0885-1">{{cite book | author = Rainsford, K. D. | title = Aspirin and related drugs | publisher = Taylor & Francis | location = Washington, DC | year = 2004 | pages = | isbn = 0-7484-0885-1 | oclc = | doi = | url = https://books.google.com/books?id=9xYuQKZSDkIC&lpg=PA67&dq=Meseclazone&pg=PA67#v=onepage&q&f=false}}</ref> It was never marketed on account of [[toxicity]] issues, namely pertaining to the [[liver]].<ref name="isbn0-7484-0885-1">{{cite book | author = Rainsford, K. D. | title = Aspirin and related drugs | publisher = Taylor & Francis | location = Washington, DC | year = 2004 | pages = | isbn = 0-7484-0885-1 | oclc = | doi = | url = https://books.google.com/books?id=9xYuQKZSDkIC&lpg=PA67&dq=Meseclazone&pg=PA67#v=onepage&q&f=false}}</ref>
