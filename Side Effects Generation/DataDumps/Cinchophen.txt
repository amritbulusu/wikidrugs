'''Cinchophen''' (trade names '''Atophan''', '''Quinophan''', and '''Phenaquin''') is an [[analgesic]] [[approved drug|drug]] that was introduced in 1910 and frequently used to treat [[gout]].  This drug is still used, in combination with [[Prednisolone]], by [[veterinarian]]s to treat arthritis in animals.

It can be prepared starting from anilin, benzaldehyde and pyruvic acid in absolute ethanol.<ref>{{Cite book|title=Intermediates for Organic Synthesis|last=Ahluwalia|first=V. K.|publisher=I. K. International|year=2005|isbn=81-88-237-33-7|location=|pages=262}}</ref>

Use of this drug in humans ceased in the 1930s due to the discovery that cinchophen can cause serious [[liver]] damage.<ref name="pmid1679861">{{cite journal |vauthors=Cutrín Prieto C, Nieto Pol E, Batalla Eiras A, Casal Iglesias L, Pérez Becerra E, Lorenzo Zúñiga V |title=[Toxic hepatitis from cinchophen: report of 3 cases] |language=Spanish|journal=Medicina clínica |volume=97 |issue=3 |pages=104–6 |year=1991 |pmid=1679861 |doi=}}</ref>

There is some evidence that it stimulates [[C-Fos]].<ref name="pmid7916144">{{cite journal |vauthors=Takayama K, Xiong Y, Miura M |title=Neuronal expression of Fos protein in the paraventricular nucleus of the hypothalamus after i.p. injection of ulcergenic cinchophen |journal=Neurosci. Lett. |volume=172 |issue=1–2 |pages=55–8 |date=May 1994 |pmid=7916144 |doi= 10.1016/0304-3940(94)90661-0|url=http://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/0304-3940(94)90661-0}}</ref>
