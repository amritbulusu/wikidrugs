<!-- Definition and medical uses -->
'''Ergocalciferol''', also known as '''vitamin D<sub>2</sub>''' and '''calciferol''', is a type of [[vitamin D]] found in food and used as a [[dietary supplement]].<ref>{{cite book|last1=Coulston|first1=Ann M.|last2=Boushey|first2=Carol|last3=Ferruzzi|first3=Mario|title=Nutrition in the Prevention and Treatment of Disease|date=2013|publisher=Academic Press|isbn=9780123918840|page=818|url=https://books.google.ca/books?id=pmapb3rvzpYC&pg=PA818|language=en}}</ref> As a supplement it is used to prevent and treat [[vitamin D deficiency]].<ref name=BNF69/> This includes vitamin D deficiency due to [[malabsorption|poor absorption by the intestines]] or [[liver disease]].<ref name=WHO2008/> It may also be used for [[hypocalcemia|low blood calcium]] due to [[hypoparathyroidism]].<ref name=WHO2008/> It is used by mouth or [[intramuscular|injection into a muscle]].<ref name=BNF69>{{cite book|title=British national formulary : BNF 69|date=2015|publisher=British Medical Association|isbn=9780857111562|pages=703–704|edition=69}}</ref><ref name=WHO2008>{{cite book|title=WHO Model Formulary 2008|date=2009|publisher=World Health Organization|isbn=9789241547659|page=498|url=http://apps.who.int/medicinedocs/documents/s16879e/s16879e.pdf|accessdate= 8 December 2016}}</ref>

<!-- Side effects and mechanism -->
Excessive doses can result in increased urine production, [[high blood pressure]], [[kidney stones]], [[kidney failure]], weakness, and constipation.<ref name=AHFS2016>{{cite web|title=Ergocalciferol|url=https://www.drugs.com/monograph/ergocalciferol.html|publisher=The American Society of Health-System Pharmacists|accessdate= 8 December 2016}}</ref> If high doses are taken for a long period of time [[tissue calcification]] may occur.<ref name=WHO2008/> It is recommended that people on high doses have their blood calcium levels regularly checked.<ref name=BNF69/> Normal doses are safe in [[pregnancy]].<ref name=Ric2015>{{cite book|last1=Hamilton|first1=Richart|title=Tarascon Pocket Pharmacopoeia 2015 Deluxe Lab-Coat Edition|date=2015|publisher=Jones & Bartlett Learning|isbn=9781284057560|page=227}}</ref> It works by increasing the amount of [[calcium]] absorbed by the intestines and kidneys.<ref name=AHFS2016/> Food in which it is found include some [[mushrooms]].<ref name=NIH2016>{{cite web|title=Office of Dietary Supplements - Vitamin D|url=https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminD-HealthProfessional/|website=ods.od.nih.gov|accessdate=30 December 2016|date=11 February 2016}}</ref>

<!-- History and culture -->
Ergocalciferol was first described in 1936.<ref name=Fis2006>{{cite book|last1=Fischer|first1=Janos|last2=Ganellin|first2=C. Robin|title=Analogue-based Drug Discovery|date=2006|publisher=John Wiley & Sons|isbn=9783527607495|page=451|url=https://books.google.ca/books?id=FjKfqkaKkAAC&pg=PA451|language=en}}</ref> It is on the [[World Health Organization's List of Essential Medicines]], the most effective and safe medicines needed in a [[health system]].<ref name=WHO19th>{{cite web|title=WHO Model List of Essential Medicines (19th List)|url=http://www.who.int/medicines/publications/essentialmedicines/EML_2015_FINAL_amended_NOV2015.pdf?ua=1|work=World Health Organization|accessdate=8 December 2016|date=April 2015}}</ref> Ergocalciferol is available as a [[generic medication]] and [[over the counter]].<ref name=AHFS2016/> In the United Kingdom a typical dose costs the [[NHS]] less than 10 pounds a month.<ref name=BNF69/> Certain foods such as [[breakfast cereal]] and [[margarine]] have ergocalciferol added to them in some countries.<ref>{{cite book|last1=Feral|first1=Priscilla|last2=Hall|first2=Lee|last3=Animals|first3=Friends of|title=Dining with Friends: The Art of North American Vegan Cuisine|date=2005|publisher=Friends of Animals/Nectar Bat Press|isbn=9780976915904|page=160|url=https://books.google.ca/books?id=kWRUJvI43MoC&pg=PA160|language=en}}</ref><ref>{{cite book|last1=Bennett|first1=Beverly|last2=Sammartano|first2=Ray|title=The Complete Idiot's Guide to Vegan Living, Second Edition|date=2012|publisher=Penguin|isbn=9781615642793|page=Chapter 15|url=https://books.google.ca/books?id=MbzIcj32RzEC&pg=PT215|language=en}}</ref>

==Medical uses==
Ergocalciferol may be used as a vitamin D [[dietary supplement|supplement]], and a 2011 clinical guideline considered ergocalciferol to be as effective as [[cholecalciferol]] (vitamin D<sub>3</sub>) which is produced naturally by the skin when exposed to ultraviolet light.<ref name="holick guideline">{{Cite journal | last1 = Holick | first1 = M. F. | last2 = Binkley | first2 = N. C. | last3 = Bischoff-Ferrari | first3 = H. A. | last4 = Gordon | first4 = C. M. | last5 = Hanley | first5 = D. A. | last6 = Heaney | first6 = R. P. | last7 = Murad | first7 = M. H. | last8 = Weaver | first8 = C. M.| doi = 10.1210/jc.2011-0385 | title = Evaluation, Treatment, and Prevention of Vitamin D Deficiency: An Endocrine Society Clinical Practice Guideline | journal = Journal of Clinical Endocrinology & Metabolism | volume = 96 | issue = 7 | pages = 1911–1930 | year = 2011 | pmid = 21646368| pmc = }}</ref>

Both forms appear to have similar efficacy in ameliorating [[rickets]]<ref>{{cite journal|pmc=3153403|year=2010|last1=Thacher|first1=TD|last2=Fischer|first2=PR|last3=Obadofin|first3=MO|last4=Levine|first4=MA|last5=Singh|first5=RJ|last6=Pettifor|first6=JM|title=Comparison of Metabolism of Vitamins D2 and D3 in Children with Nutritional Rickets|volume=25|issue=9|pages=1988–1995|doi=10.1002/jbmr.99|journal=Journal of Bone and Mineral Research|pmid=20499377}}</ref> and reducing the incidence of falls in elderly patients.<ref>{{Cite journal | last1 = Fosnight | first1 = S. M. | last2 = Zafirau | first2 = W. J. | last3 = Hazelett | first3 = S. E. | doi = 10.1592/phco.28.2.225 | title = Vitamin D Supplementation to Prevent Falls in the Elderly: Evidence and Practical Considerations | journal = Pharmacotherapy | volume = 28 | issue = 2 | pages = 225–234 | year = 2008 | pmid = 18225968 | pmc = }}</ref>

==Mechanism==
Ergocalciferol is a [[secosteroid]] formed by a [[photochemistry|photochemical]] bond breaking of a [[steroid]], specifically, by the action of [[ultraviolet light]] on [[ergosterol]].

==Names==
Viosterol, the name given to early preparations of irradiated ergosterol, is essentially synonymous with ergocalciferol.<ref>Science Service (1930) Viosterol official name for irradiated ergosterol, J. Chem. Educ. 7(1) 166, DOI: 10.1021/ed007p166, see [pubs.acs.org/doi/abs/10.1021/ed007p166, accessed 10 July 2014.</ref><ref>See "Viosterol" and "Calciferol" at Merriam-Webster Medical Dictionary, e.g., [http://www.merriam-webster.com/medical/viosterol] and [http://www.merriam-webster.com/medical/calciferol], accessed 10 July 2014.</ref>

Ergocalciferol is manufactured and marketed under various names, including Deltalin ([[Eli Lilly and Company]]), Drisdol (Sanofi-Synthelabo) and Calcidol (Patrin Pharma).
