'''Padimate O''' is an [[organic compound]] related to the water-soluble compound [[4-Aminobenzoic acid|PABA]] (4-aminobenzoic acid) that is used as an ingredient in some [[sunscreen]]s.  This yellowish water-insoluble oily liquid is an [[ester]] formed by the [[condensation reaction|condensation]] of [[2-Ethylhexanol|2-ethylhexanol]] with [[dimethylaminobenzoic acid]].  Other names for padimate O include 2-ethylhexyl 4-dimethylaminobenzoate, Escalol 507, octyldimethyl PABA, and OD-PABA.

==Photobiology==
Padimate O absorbs [[ultraviolet]] rays, thereby preventing [[direct DNA damage]] by UV-B. However, the thus-excited padimate O molecule may then react with DNA to produce [[indirect DNA damage]], similar to the effects of ionizing radiation. An ''[[in vitro]]'' yeast study conducted in 1993 demonstrated the sunlight-induced [[mutagen]]icity of padimate O.<ref name=Knowland1993>{{cite journal |doi=10.1016/0014-5793(93)80141-G |title=Sunlight-induced mutagenicity of a common sunscreen ingredient |year=1993 |last1=Knowland |first1=John |last2=McKenzie |first2=Edward A |last3=McHugh |first3=Peter J |last4=Cridland |first4=Nigel A |journal=FEBS Letters |volume=324 |issue=3 |pages=309–13 |pmid=8405372}}</ref>  The photobiological properties of padimate O resemble those of [[Michler's ketone]], which is considered photocarcinogenic in rats and mice. These findings suggest that padimate O might also be photocarcinogenic.<ref name=Sensitization>{{cite journal |doi=10.1016/S1383-5718(99)00091-1 |title=Illumination of human keratinocytes in the presence of the sunscreen ingredient Padimate-O and through an SPF-15 sunscreen reduces direct photodamage to DNA but increases strand breaks |year=1999 |last1=Gulston |first1=Melanie |last2=Knowland |first2=John |journal=Mutation Research/Genetic Toxicology and Environmental Mutagenesis |volume=444 |pages=49–60 |pmid=10477339}}</ref>

However, multiple ''[[in vivo]]'' studies conducted in hairless mice following topical application of padimate O have demonstrated no carcinogenic effects and that padimate O reduces the number of and delays the appearance of UV-induced skin tumors.<ref>{{cite journal |doi=10.1016/S0190-9622(80)80221-0 |title=Sunscreens prevent ultraviolet photocarcinogenesis |year=1980 |last1=Kligman |first1=Lorraine H. |last2=Akin |first2=Frank J. |last3=Kligman |first3=Albert M. |journal=Journal of the American Academy of Dermatology |volume=3 |pages=30–5 |pmid=6967495 |issue=1}}</ref><ref>{{cite journal |doi=10.1016/1011-1344(91)80169-I |title=Time-dependent decrease in sunscreen protection against chronic photodamage in UVB-irradiated hairless mouse skin |year=1991 |last1=Bissett |first1=Donald L. |last2=McBride |first2=James F. |last3=Hannon |first3=Daniel P. |last4=Patrick |first4=Larry F. |journal=Journal of Photochemistry and Photobiology B: Biology |volume=9 |issue=3–4 |pages=323–334}}</ref><ref>{{cite journal |doi=10.1016/S0190-9622(96)90677-5 |title=Synergistic topical photoprotection by a combination of the iron chelator 2-furildioxime and sunscreen |year=1996 |last1=Bissett |first1=Donald L |last2=McBride |first2=James F |journal=Journal of the American Academy of Dermatology |volume=35 |issue=4 |pages=546–9 |pmid=8859281}}</ref><ref>{{cite journal |doi=10.1016/S0027-5107(98)00188-2 |title=The effects of two UVB radiation-absorbing sunscreens on UV radiation-induced carcinogenesis, suppression of the contact hypersensitivity response and histological changes in the hairless mouse |year=1998 |last1=Kerr |first1=Caroline |journal=Mutation Research/Fundamental and Molecular Mechanisms of Mutagenesis |volume=422 |pages=161–4 |pmid=9920441 |issue=1}}</ref>
