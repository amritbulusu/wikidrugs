'''Demoxytocin''' ([[International Nonproprietary Name|INN]]) (brand names '''Sandopart''', '''Odeax''', '''Sandopral'''), also known as '''desaminooxytocin''' or '''deaminooxytocin''', as well as '''1-(3-mercaptopropanoic acid)oxytocin''' ('''<nowiki>[</nowiki>Mpa<sup>1</sup><nowiki>]</nowiki>OT'''), is an [[oxytocic]] [[peptide]] [[drug]] that is used to [[labor induction|induce labor]],<ref name="Bladon2002" /> [[galactogogue|promote lactation]],<ref name="MortonHall2012" /> and to prevent and treat [[puerperal]] (postpartum) [[mastitis]] (breast inflammation).<ref name="pmid468056">{{cite journal | vauthors = Sternadel Z, Gerkowicz J | title = [Use of deaminooxytocin (Sandopart) in the prevention and treatment of puerperal mastitis] | language = Polish | journal = Ginekol. Pol. | volume = 50 | issue = 5 | pages = 413–6 | year = 1979 | pmid = 468056 | doi = | url = }}</ref> Demoxytocin is a [[chemical synthesis|synthetic]] [[structural analog|analogue]] of [[oxytocin]] and has similar activities,<ref name="BoxtelSantoso2008">{{cite book|author1=C.J. van Boxtel|author2=B. Santoso|author3=I.R. Edwards|title=Drug Benefits and Risks: International Textbook of Clinical Pharmacology - Revised 2nd Edition|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=idbvAgAAQBAJ&pg=PA389|date=6 August 2008|publisher=IOS Press|isbn=978-1-60750-345-3|pages=389–}}</ref> but is more potent and has a longer [[half-life]] in comparison.<ref name="MortonHall2012" /><ref name="Bladon2002" /> Unlike oxytocin, which is given via [[intravenous injection]], demoxytocin is administered as a [[buccal administration|buccal]] [[tablet (pharmacy)|tablet]] formulation.<ref name="MortonHall2012" /><ref name="EskesFinster2013">{{cite book|author1=Tom K. A. B. Eskes|author2=Mieczyslaw Finster|title=Drug Therapy During Pregnancy|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=SAMlBQAAQBAJ&pg=PA185|date=22 October 2013|publisher=Elsevier|isbn=978-1-4831-6298-0|pages=185–}}</ref>

The drug was first synthesized in 1960 and was introduced into clinical practice in 1971 by [[Sandoz]].<ref name="Elks2014" /><ref name="GrossMeienhofer2014">{{cite book|author1=Erhard Gross|author2=Johannes Meienhofer|title=Major Methods of Peptide Bond Formation: The Peptides Analysis, Synthesis, Biology|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=vz2aBQAAQBAJ&pg=PA53|date=10 May 2014|publisher=Elsevier|isbn=978-1-4832-1796-3|pages=53–}}</ref> It is marketed in several [[Europe]]an countries, including [[Italy]], [[Czechoslovakia]], and [[Poland]].<ref>{{cite book|title=Index Nominum 2000: International Drug Directory|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=5GpcTQD_L2oC&pg=PA301|date=January 2000|publisher=Taylor & Francis|isbn=978-3-88763-075-1|pages=301–}}</ref><ref name="Elks2014">{{cite book|author=J. Elks|title=The Dictionary of Drugs: Chemical Data: Chemical Data, Structures and Bibliographies|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=0vXTBwAAQBAJ&pg=PA349|date=14 November 2014|publisher=Springer|isbn=978-1-4757-2085-3|pages=349–}}</ref><ref name="MortonHall2012">{{cite book|author1=I.K. Morton|author2=Judith M. Hall|title=Concise Dictionary of Pharmacological Agents: Properties and Synonyms|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=tsjrCAAAQBAJ&pg=PA93|date=6 December 2012|publisher=Springer Science & Business Media|isbn=978-94-011-4439-1|pages=93–}}</ref> It has the [[amino acid sequence]] Mpa-Tyr-Ile-Gln-Asn-Cys-Pro-Leu-Gly-NH<sub>2</sub> (Mpa = β-mercaptopropionic acid),<ref name="Bladon2002">{{cite book|author=Christine Bladon|title=Pharmaceutical Chemistry: Therapeutic Aspects of Biomacromolecules|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=y3JTZtHExicC&pg=PA61|date=3 April 2002|publisher=John Wiley & Sons|isbn=978-0-471-49637-3|pages=61–}}</ref> and is an analogue of oxytocin wherein the leading [[cysteine]] is replaced with [[β-mercaptopropionic acid]].<ref name="Bladon2002" />
