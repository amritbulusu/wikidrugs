'''Crufomate''' is an insecticide.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://pubchem.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/compound/Crufomate|title=Crufomate {{!}} C12H19ClNO3P - PubChem|last=Pubchem|website=pubchem.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov|access-date=2016-05-20}}</ref>

Potentially toxic to humans, the [[National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health]] has set a time-weighted average [[Occupational exposure limit|exposure limit]] of 5 milligrams per cubic meter, with a short-term limit of up to 20 milligrams per cubic meter for brief exposures of no longer than 15 minutes.<ref name=":0">{{Cite web|url=https://www.cdc.gov/niosh/npg/npgd0158.html|title=CDC - NIOSH Pocket Guide to Chemical Hazards - Crufomate|publisher=Centers for Disease Control and Prevention |date=11 April 2016 |access-date=20 May 2016}}</ref>

Routes of exposure to crufomate include [[Inhalation exposure|inhalation]], eye contact, [[Absorption (skin)|skin absorption]], and [[ingestion]]. Symptoms include eye irritation, [[Anorexia (symptom)|loss of appetite]], [[nausea]], [[diarrhea]], [[Abdominal pain|abdominal cramps]], [[perspiration]], [[Tears|lacrimation]], [[blurred vision]], [[dyspnea]], [[Wheeze|wheezing]], and respiratory and skin irritation. First aid measures include artificial respiration to treat inhalation exposures, eye irrigation for eye exposures, and immediate washing with soap for skin exposure. Immediate medical attention should be sought if crufomate is swallowed.<ref name=":0" />
