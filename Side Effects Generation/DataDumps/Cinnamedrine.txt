'''Cinnamedrine''' ([[International Nonproprietary Name|INN]], [[United States Adopted Name|USAN]]), also known as '''''N''-cinnamylephedrine''', is a [[sympathomimetic]] [[drug]] with similar effects relative to those of [[ephedrine]].<ref name="Elks2014">{{cite book|author=J. Elks|title=The Dictionary of Drugs: Chemical Data: Chemical Data, Structures and Bibliographies|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=0vXTBwAAQBAJ&pg=PA279|date=14 November 2014|publisher=Springer|isbn=978-1-4757-2085-3|pages=279–}}</ref><ref name="MortonHall2012">{{cite book|author1=I.K. Morton|author2=Judith M. Hall|title=Concise Dictionary of Pharmacological Agents: Properties and Synonyms|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=tsjrCAAAQBAJ&pg=PA77|date=6 December 2012|publisher=Springer Science & Business Media|isbn=978-94-011-4439-1|pages=77–}}</ref> It also has some [[local anesthetic]] activity.<ref name="MortonHall2012" /> Cinnamedrine was previously used, in combination with [[analgesic]]s, as an [[antispasmodic]] to treat [[dysmenorrhea]] in the [[over-the-counter]] [[drug]] [[Midol]] in the 1980s.<ref name="MortonHall2012" /><ref name="Publishing2013">{{cite book|author=William Andrew Publishing|title=Pharmaceutical Manufacturing Encyclopedia, 3rd Edition|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=_J2ti4EkYpkC&pg=PA1027|date=22 October 2013|publisher=Elsevier|isbn=978-0-8155-1856-3|pages=1027–}}</ref> There is a [[case report]] of the drug being [[drug abuse|abuse]]d as a [[psychostimulant]].<ref name="pmid6887304">{{cite journal | vauthors = Fellows KW, Giannini AJ | title = Cinnamedrine: potential for abuse | journal = J. Toxicol. Clin. Toxicol. | volume = 20 | issue = 1 | pages = 93–9 | year = 1983 | pmid = 6887304 | doi = | url = }}</ref>
