'''Sulfiram''' ([[International Nonproprietary Name|INN]]) or '''monosulfiram''', trade name '''Tetmosol''', is an [[ectoparasiticide]] used in the treatment and prevention of [[scabies]].<ref name=Martindale>{{cite book |editor=Sweetman, Sean C. |chapter=Pesticides and repellents |title=Martindale: the complete drug reference |edition=36th |year=2009 |page=2050 |publisher=Pharmaceutical Press |location=London |isbn=978-0-85369-840-1}}</ref> It is usually sold as a solution or medicated [[soap]], sometimes in combination with [[benzyl benzoate]].

Sulfiram is now rarely used, but, {{as of|2015|lc=yes}}, is still available in Brazil, India, and South Africa (as monotherapy).<ref name=Martindale/>

==Adverse effects==
Dizziness, headache, fatigue and [[erythema]]tous [[drug eruption|rash]] may occur.<ref>{{cite web |author=[No authors listed] |url=http://www.medicinanet.com.br/bula/4627/sarfiram.htm |title=Sarfiram - Bula |year=2009 |work=Bulário de Remédios Comerciais |publisher=MedicinaNET |language=Portuguese |accessdate=2010-08-11}}</ref> A single case of [[toxic epidermal necrolysis]] was reported in 1968.<ref>{{cite journal |author=Copeman PW |title=Toxic epidermal necrolysis caused by skin hypersensitivity to monosulfiram |journal=[[British Medical Journal]] |volume=1 |issue=5592 |pages=623–4 |date=March 1968 |pmid=5637574 |pmc=1985336 |doi=10.1136/bmj.1.5592.623}}</ref>

Sulfiram is structurally related to [[disulfiram]] (Antabuse), and readily converts to disulfiram when exposed to light. Like disulfiram, it can produce an [[disulfiram#Interaction with alcohol|unpleasant reaction when consumed with alcohol]].<ref name=Martindale/><ref>{{cite journal |vauthors=Mays DC, Nelson AN, Benson LM, Johnson KL, Naylor S, Lipsky JJ |title=Photolysis of sulfiram: a mechanism for its disulfiram-like reaction |journal=[[Biochemical Pharmacology (journal)|Biochemical Pharmacology]] |volume=48 |issue=10 |pages=1917–25 |date=November 1994 |pmid=7986203 |doi=10.1016/0006-2952(94)90590-8}}</ref>
