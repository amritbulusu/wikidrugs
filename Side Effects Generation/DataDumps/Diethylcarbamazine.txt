<!-- Definition and medical uses -->
'''Diethylcarbamazine''' ('''DEC''') is a medication used in the treatment of [[filariasis]] including [[lymphatic filariasis]], [[tropical pulmonary eosinophilia]], and [[loiasis]].<ref name=CDC2016/> It may also be used for prevention of loiasis in those at high risk.<ref name=CDC2016>{{cite web|title=Our Formulary Infectious Diseases Laboratories CDC|url=https://www.cdc.gov/laboratory/drugservice/formulary.html|website=www.cdc.gov|accessdate=7 December 2016|date=22 September 2016|deadurl=no|archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20161216173833/https://www.cdc.gov/laboratory/drugservice/formulary.html|archivedate=16 December 2016|df=}}</ref> While it has been used for [[onchocerciasis]] (river blindness), [[invermectin]] is preferred.<ref name=WHO2008/> It is taken by mouth.<ref name=ERC2014/>

<!-- Side effects and mechanism -->
Common side effects itching, facial swelling, headaches, and feeling tired.<ref name=Drug2016/> Other side effects include [[vision loss]] and [[dizziness]].<ref name=Drug2016>{{cite web|title=Diethylcarbamazine Advanced Patient Information - Drugs.com|url=https://www.drugs.com/cons/diethylcarbamazine.html|website=www.drugs.com|accessdate=8 December 2016|deadurl=no|archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20161220223430/https://www.drugs.com/cons/diethylcarbamazine.html|archivedate=20 December 2016|df=}}</ref> It is a recommended treatment in [[pregnancy]] and appears to be safe for the baby.<ref>{{cite book|last1=Sweet|first1=Richard L.|last2=Gibbs|first2=Ronald S.|title=Infectious Diseases of the Female Genital Tract|date=2009|publisher=Lippincott Williams & Wilkins|isbn=9780781778152|page=382|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=wuR_ngItU5oC&pg=PA382|language=en|deadurl=no|archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20170910145628/https://books.google.com/books?id=wuR_ngItU5oC&pg=PA382|archivedate=2017-09-10|df=}}</ref><ref>{{cite book|last1=Herbert-Ashton|first1=Marilyn|last2=Clarkson|first2=Nancy Elaine|title=Quick Look Nursing: Pharmacology|date=2005|publisher=Jones & Bartlett Learning|isbn=9780763735951|page=48|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=RuyTlze9Y5kC&pg=PA48|language=en|deadurl=no|archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20170910145628/https://books.google.com/books?id=RuyTlze9Y5kC&pg=PA48|archivedate=2017-09-10|df=}}</ref> The [[World Health Organization]]; however, recommends until after pregnancy for treatment.<ref name=WHO2008>{{cite book|title=WHO Model Formulary 2008|date=2009|publisher=World Health Organization|isbn=9789241547659|page=91|url=http://apps.who.int/medicinedocs/documents/s16879e/s16879e.pdf|deadurl=no|archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20161213060118/http://apps.who.int/medicinedocs/documents/s16879e/s16879e.pdf|archivedate=2016-12-13|df=}}</ref> It is made from [[piperazine]].<ref>{{cite web|title=WHO Model Prescribing Information: Drugs Used in Parasitic Diseases - Second Edition: Helminths: Lymphatic filariasis: Diethylcarbamazine|url=http://apps.who.int/medicinedocs/en/d/Jh2922e/3.5.1.html|website=apps.who.int|accessdate=8 December 2016|page=152|date=1995|deadurl=no|archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20161120192136/http://apps.who.int/medicinedocs/en/d/Jh2922e/3.5.1.html|archivedate=20 November 2016|df=}}</ref>

<!-- History and culture -->
Diethylcarbamazine was discovered in 1947.<ref>{{cite book|last1=Busvine|first1=James|title=Disease Transmission by Insects: Its Discovery and 90 Years of Effort to Prevent it|date=2012|publisher=Springer Science & Business Media|isbn=9783642457166|page=260|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=POnvCAAAQBAJ&pg=PA260|language=en|deadurl=no|archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20170910145628/https://books.google.com/books?id=POnvCAAAQBAJ&pg=PA260|archivedate=2017-09-10|df=}}</ref> It is on the [[World Health Organization's List of Essential Medicines]], the most effective and safe medicines needed in a [[health system]].<ref name=WHO19th>{{cite web|title=WHO Model List of Essential Medicines (19th List)|url=http://www.who.int/medicines/publications/essentialmedicines/EML_2015_FINAL_amended_NOV2015.pdf?ua=1|work=World Health Organization|accessdate=8 December 2016|date=April 2015|deadurl=no|archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20161213052708/http://www.who.int/medicines/publications/essentialmedicines/EML_2015_FINAL_amended_NOV2015.pdf?ua=1|archivedate=13 December 2016|df=}}</ref> The wholesale cost in the [[developing world]] is less than 0.01 USD per dose.<ref name=ERC2014>{{cite web|title=Diethylcarbamazine Citrate|url=http://erc.msh.org/dmpguide/resultsdetail.cfm?language=english&code=DIE50T&s_year=2014&year=2014&str=50%20mg&desc=Diethylcarbamazine%20Citrate&pack=new&frm=TAB-CAP&rte=PO&class_code2=06%2E1%2E2%2E&supplement=&class_name=%2806%2E1%2E2%2E%29Antifilarials%3Cbr%3E|website=International Drug Price Indicator Guide|accessdate=1 December 2016}}</ref> It is not commercially available in the United States but can be acquired from the [[Center for Disease Control]].<ref name=CDC2016/>

==Medical uses==
DEC is indicated for treatment of individual patients with certain [[Filariasis|filaria]]l [[disease]]s. These diseases include: [[lymphatic filariasis]] caused by [[infection]] with ''[[Wuchereria bancrofti]]'', ''[[Brugia malayi]]'', or ''[[Brugia timori]]''; tropical [[pulmonary eosinophilia]], and [[loiasis]].

In cases of [[onchocerciasis]], another common filarial parasite, the drug is not used. This is because of the intense and unbearable itching associated with the dead subcutaneous parasites.

DEC continues to be the mainstay for treatment of patients with lymphatic filariasis and loiasis. DEC is also used to prevent [[Dirofilaria immitis|heartworm]] in dogs.

Now WHO recommends prescribing DEC to patients who are infected with microfilariae of filarial parasite and also to control transmission of infection in filariasis endemic areas.

Contraindications are previous history of heart problems, gastrointestinal problems, and allergy.{{medcn|date=September 2015}}

==Mechanism==
DEC is an inhibitor of [[arachidonic acid]] metabolism in [[microfilaria]]. This makes the microfilariae more susceptible to innate immune attack, but does not kill the parasites outright.<ref>{{Cite journal | pmid = 20599991| year = 2010| author1 = El-Shahawi| first1 = G.
