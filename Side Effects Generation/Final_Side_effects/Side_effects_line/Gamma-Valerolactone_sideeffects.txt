GVL behaves as a [[prodrug]] to [[gamma-Hydroxyvaleric acid|γ-hydroxyvaleric acid]] (GHV), a [[drug]] with similar effects to those of [[gamma-Hydroxybutyric acid|γ-hydroxybutyric acid]] (GHB), albeit with less potentcy in comparison.<ref name="pmid23486087">{{cite journal | vauthors = Andresen-Streichert H, Jungen H, Gehl A, Müller A, Iwersen-Bergmann S | title = Uptake of gamma-valerolactone--detection of gamma-hydroxyvaleric acid in human urine samples | journal = J Anal Toxicol | volume = 37 | issue = 4 | pages = 250–4 | year = 2013 | pmid = 23486087 | doi = 10.1093/jat/bkt013 | url = }}</ref> Because GHB is controlled in many parts of the world, while GVL is not, GVL has gained popularity as a legal substitute for GHB.<ref name="pmid23486087" /><ref name="Smith2004">{{cite book|author=Fred Smith|title=Handbook of Forensic Drug Analysis|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=8JsQgRO3QcwC&pg=PA462|date=31 December 2004|publisher=Academic Press|isbn=978-0-08-047289-8|pages=462–}}</ref>
